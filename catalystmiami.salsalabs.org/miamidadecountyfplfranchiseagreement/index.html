We would like to see FPL produce enough renewable energy to serve 100% of the energy needs of unincorporated Miami-Dade County by 2050. If this agreement was limited to 10 years, we would want to see FPL produce enough renewable energy to serve 33.3% of the energy needs of unincorporated Miami-Dade County by 2030. There are five cities in the U.S. that are currently powered by 100% renewable energy, such as Georgetown, TX and Greensburg, KS. Seventy-five cities, six counties and one state (Hawaii) are working on transitioning to renewable energy now.

According to the FY 2016-17 Adopted Budget, the County’s weatherization (home improvement/energy efficiency) program has serviced an average of 78 homes a year since 2014. FPL spent $89,000 on low-income energy efficiency programs in Miami in 2015 - the lowest out of the 51 largest metropolitan area in the United States according to a July 2017 study by ACEEE. We would like Miami-Dade County and FPL to establish a partnership to expand the County’s existing weatherization program, which includes AC improvement, to serve a minimum of 1,000 households per year.

Modal title
As we saw after Hurricane Irma, the susceptibility of our power facilities to natural disasters can mean life or death for our community members. We would like to see a collaborative annual review between FPL and MDC to consider infrastructure vulnerabilities due to climate change, including hurricanes, sea level rise, and extreme heat. This should be done for the purpose of identifying and implementing necessary improvements. Assessing the vulnerability of the FPL and County infrastructure will ensure that dollars invested in infrastructure are spent wisely, and that our communities remain healthy and safe.
Modal title
Miami-Dade’s last contract with FPL was signed in 1989. In 1989, solar panels (modules) cost approximately $8 per watt of power. By 2014, that price dropped to $0.40 per watt. Between 2009 and 2018, the cost of solar electricity in the United States fallen by 85 percent. Who knows what technological advancements we will come across in the solar energy field in the next 30 years? Why lock ourselves into a long term contract? Additionally, this contract should ensure accountability. Shortening the contract to 10 years, with a review of progress towards milestones conducted every two years, will ensure that goals are being met in a timely fashion.

Take Action

Every year, Miami-Dade County residents pay an additional $27 million on our electricity bills to Florida Power & Light, which then gets passed through to the County. This pass-through tax was set up in 1989 through the County's contract, aka franchise agreement, with FPL. Through this agreement, FPL gains the right to use County property to deliver electricity. Coming up in 2020, the County will renegotiate this agreement. 

When our last franchise agreement with FPL was signed in 1989, cell phones were the size of a brick, and the world wide web was just invented. Since then, technology has advanced, especially in the energy sector. Solar power is on track to be the most affordable source of power in many parts of the world. However, Miamians are being kept out of the solar revolution, high energy bills cause financial stress for families countywide, and our grid is still unreliable during storms.

Join our coalition, Miamians for Energy Freedom, in urging elected officials to stand up for Miami-Dade County residents. We deserve a contract that provides significant community benefits and helps propel us to a more renewable and affordable energy future. Once the contract terms are negotiated, we will have the right to vote on it.

Subject: It's time we get our money's worth through our franchise agreement with FPL
Message:
We all know that Florida is the sunshine state. However, our state's monopoly utilities are upholding barriers that keep us stuck in the past. We lag behind smaller, cloudier regions in terms of solar. Data needed by County staff to move our region towards a clean energy future is not shared. Energy efficiency programs available to our most under-resourced community members have the lowest investment in the nation. Despite billions spent on hardening our power grid, in 2017 too many in our County lost power for several days due to a category 1 hurricane.

The County is renegotiating its contract, also known as franchise agreement, with FPL for the first time since 1989. Undoubtedly, it is time to move beyond the status quo and give Miami-Dade County the freedom to move towards a more resilient and renewable energy future. Remember that our County made the commitment to be one of the world's 100 Resilient Cities. I ask that you use your influence to ensure that the renewed franchise agreement with FPL provides the following benefits:

- Aggressive renewable energy goals, such as 100% of our electricity coming from renewable sources
- Construction of a community solar installation in Miami-Dade County that provides a direct benefit to working class and low-income FPL customers in the County, with a specific goal of meaningfully reducing their energy burden
- Establish a partnership where our County's existing low-income weatherization programs are expanded to include energy efficiency and upgraded HVAC systems with the goal of serving a minimum of 1,000 households per year
- A collaborative annual review with MDC regarding infrastructure vulnerability to sea level rise, projected heat index, hurricanes, storm surge, and other climate considerations for the purpose of identifying and implementing necessary adaptations
- Limit the term of this franchise agreement to no more than 10 years in order to maintain both accountability and flexibility
- Dedicate the funds acquired by the County through the franchise fee to renewable energy initiatives, energy assistance, and/or community resiliency initiatives specific to post-hurricane energy access for vulnerable communities.

I appreciate your time and hope you will consider my request.
